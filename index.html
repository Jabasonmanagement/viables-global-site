The message

A letter on social justice in Nigeria

This is a public letter, offered as a mirror and a conversation starter. You can keep it as-is, translate it, or adapt it‚Äîwith credit‚Äîinto your own community work.

Dear Nigeria,

There is a version of you that lives in our memories‚Äîof laughter in crowded compounds, of neighbours who knew our names, of streets that felt rough but familiar. And there is the version we wake up to now: headlines of corruption, police brutality, poverty, unpaid salaries, and a system that often rewards the loudest and punishes the honest.

Somewhere between those two Nigerias are millions of ordinary people doing their best: parents stretching food and school fees, young people learning skills in difficult economies, voters standing under the sun, and citizens who still whisper, ‚ÄúIt can be better‚Äù even when everything says otherwise.

My message is simple but not soft: every Nigerian life must matter more than power, tribe, religion, or politics. Until a poor person can walk the same road as a senator without fear, we have work to do. Until our young people no longer have to choose between japa and survival, we have work to do.

Social justice is not a slogan; it is small, consistent courage. It is the okada rider refusing to pay a bribe, the civil servant quietly doing honest work, the protester demanding better without losing their humanity, the artist telling truth in music, in film, in books.

I don‚Äôt have all the answers. But I believe this: if we keep insisting that the system must work for the weakest Nigerian, not just the strongest, something will shift. Justice is slow, but it listens when people refuse to be silent.

If these words find you tired, let them also find you seen. You are not alone in your anger, in your disappointment, or in your hope. Nigeria is not a problem to be escaped; it is a home to be rebuilt, one fair choice at a time.

With stubborn hope,
JAB Aladeselu

Visual story

Seeing justice in three frames

For people who may not read the full letter, these three visual ideas capture the heart of the message: from daily struggle to thoughtful pause to collective action.

Frame 1

The everyday struggle

A crowded Lagos bus stop, a market in Ibadan, a village road in the North. Nigerians carrying the weight of bad systems on strong shoulders. This frame says: we see the injustice you live with daily.

Frame 2

The pause for truth

A quiet room, a conversation, a moment where we stop performing and start telling the truth: about corruption, about fear, about the cost of silence. Justice begins when we stop pretending.

Frame 3

The choice to act

Youths organising, communities voting, neighbours protecting each other, professionals refusing to abuse their office. Change in Nigeria will not be magic; it will be these small, courageous choices, repeated.
